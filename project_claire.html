Personal Project - 2020

Claire Boutique App

Personal

Overview

Claire is the poster child of fun, sophisticated and instagram-able styles that target the new "online" generation. Their boutiques have become a huge hit but they want to keep up with their customers who are used to having everything literally at their fingertip.

The aim of this project is to create the digital presence for Claire, an app that adheres to their brand image.

My Role

Entire product design from research to conception, visualization and testing

My Team

As this was a personal project, I was the only person working on this

Timeline

June 2020; 8 weeks

The Challenge

Design Claire's online journey - The new motto of Claire is FFE (Fun, Fast and Easy) and they want an app that translates this and and a shopping experience that excites the user. So the challenge here is to create an online store for Claire that makes shopping quick and easy.

The Goal

Claire wanted to focus on their long-term goals and the main one was to move the shopping experience online. They are well known for their quick and easy shopping experience and they wanted that experience translated into their online shopping app.

Term Goal: Quick and easy shopping experience

User Research & Insights

With the company goal and assumptions in mind, I conducted user interviews with online shoppers and boutique shoppers in the age range of 16 - 35 to get an idea of what they would expect from a shopping app. From the responses, I created an experience map stating the main functionalities, goals and expectations that the users have. From this I derived the main interview insights and compared it with the company goal.

Users didn't want to be compelled to create an account in the app just to purchase. They all love a "Guest" option.

"I don't want to create an account for one-time shopping"

Everyone wanted a good categorization of the catalogs.

"Good categorization of stuff so I can find things easily"

A set of people come to the app with a specific sort of dress in mind. They mostly have to filter out or browse till they find that. They needed an easy way to find a specific kind of dress they had in mind.

"I usually go to the app to buy things and not browse things"

Many stated that they always had problems with the size of the dress. Since they could not try on the dress and the same size number means different things for different brands, many had refer the size chart multiple times to check which corresponds to their size

"Good size conversion, small in one brand does not mean the same in another"

Journey Map

While defining the journey map with the steps for customers to complete a shopping task, I identified 2 major paths for the actors (Customer) to reach their goal of getting a new dress. Creating this map helped me visualize the journey of the customer through the service and to narrow down the broad target to specific ones that I can focus on solving. Discussing and refining the journey map gave me 4 touch points.

Quick browse through the app: users have an interface to quickly and easily browse through the options they have.


Find dress & size: the user should be able to find the dress they have in mind and the correct size.


Take a picture of a dress to find similar: Find out if the same dress or a similar dress is available.


Buy online: Online buying and delivering like any other e-commerce site. Reliable and easy couch shopping.

Personas

From this study, I could further segment the main users into 2 groups: users who can afford to spend time browsing through the shop catalog and those who come to the shop for a specific goal.

Wireframing, Prototyping, Usability-Testing & Iterations

Using the insights I gathered from the research, I began creating user flows. I started creating the information architecture and low-fi concepts for primary use cases.

I did a quick session with the users to see if the information architecture for the online shopping app made sense to them.

Version 1

Version 2

With low-fidelity wireframe sketches, the general structure of the application could easily be tested in usability tests. Without much effort, adjustments could be made before starting the costlier digital implementation.

After some adjustments on the sketched wireframe, a clickable prototype was created in Figma. Again, user tests revealed small vulnerabilities in the structure of the user interface, in some formulations and interactions. Along the way, the users gave some awesome suggestions and asked some questions that we had not thought about, which helped to enhance the prototype.

My Learnings

  1. Design is all about solving a problem: I got to learn that design is not about the UI, itâ€™s about the problem that you are solving for the users.
  2. Always start with a long term goal: The long term goal of the solution gives you an idea about how to approach the interview questions. If unsure of how to start a new project, the first step can be noting down the long term goal you want to achieve through your solution.
  3. The Map is my new best friend: It can be frustrating to figure out where to proceed once you have talked to the users. I found that making a map about how the actor (user) reaches the goal helps to get an idea about the user flow and the points I need to focus on.
  4. A sketch is worth more than a prototype: I made the mistake of jumping directly into the high fidelity prototype in the beginning. while doing a usability test with that, thing went downhill and I had to start over with the design process. This made me realize that usability testing with a sketch is easier, gives more insights and saves a lot of time in the long run.
  5. Your first idea is never your best idea: Learn to let go of ideas as new insights and user feedbacks comes along.
