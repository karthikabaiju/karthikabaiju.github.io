Team Project - 2021

Vet's Pocket

Freelance

Overview

As field veterinarians are constantly on the move, they need a solution that allows them to easily add patient details on the go. With limited access to desktop computers and little time to enter data on laptops, they often resort to inefficient and outdated methods. To address this need, our team developed a pocket-friendly solution that allows vets to manage their appointments, records, and inventory on the go. This solution is designed to help doctors save 2-3 hours per week on patient and inventory management, allowing them to spend more time providing care to their animal patients.

My Role

Researcher and Designer

My Team

Timeline

September 2020 - November 2021

The Challenge

How can field vets get an effective way to keep track of their patient data which is easy to use, portable and easy on the pocket.

Field veterinarians are always on the move, so they have a hard time using traditional hospital management software. The few available mobile versions are either too expensive for a new vet just starting in the field or just a big nightmare to use (you end up chucking it to the corner of your app list). Even though there are software options available for traditional hospital management and pet owners, there is no proper one that works on a budget, on the field vets.

What were the Vets using before?

They tend to use which ever option allows them the most flexibility while on the field,

  1. OneDrive
  2. Excel/Google Sheets
  3. Good old pen and paper

Research

A look into what's out there.

I scoured the internet and App Store thinking there must be something that can help. This is a very common requirement for all in the Vet industry. Most of the options I came across were either expense for a new person on the field (Digitail, VitusVet), desktop apps for big hospitals or clinics too focused on pets or simply were a nightmare to use (Animal Record On the Go).

Getting on the field to talk to real users revealed a lot of information.

I went out into the field to see what the users had to say. I interviewed 5 vets who are field vets, that mean they do not work in the clinic but are on the move most of the times. The interview pool included candidates that are worked in the field to more multiple years as well as those that newly graduated from college. From the data I collected, I created an ideal experience map to capture the current user journey and pain pointed.

Figuring out the major touchpoints.

From the experience map, I was able to figure out the major touch points where the user will be turning to the app while they are on-site or before.

  1. Add in the appointment and the initial data
  2. Preparation and taking patient history
  3. Patient records
  4. At the appointment, to record observations, treatments, and billing
  5. To check what all materials they have on hand at the moment

Design Goal

Create an easy, light, user-friendly, and affordable patient and visit management system for field vets.

The functionalities required by field vets are entirely different from those required by vets in clinics and hospitals. All the applications in the market are designed for hospitals and clinics, which makes these solutions expensive. Field vets usually work alone or in small numbers, so they require a more portable solution.

Ideation

How would the user journey for this problem look?

The experience map later developed into a journey map, to have an idea of how I imagined my app to pan out and what my desired user experience would entail.

Appointment: On getting a call about a patient, the users have to either schedule an appointment (need to note down the place and time) or have an online consultation (note down the observations, treatments, and billing).


Patient history: Most owners will not have the animal history in hand. So the user will have to take the patient history initially. Since most on the field vets work alone, they will mostly be taking the history and observations orally. Also, the users need a place to keep track of the patient history they recorded plus the ones they treated.


Visit: Note down all the observations, treatment, and billing for a visit. Have them stored along with the patient. Schedule a follow-up appointment for the visit.


Inventory: Keep track of medical items that the user has in hand and what all they have used.

During ideation, we identified two personas for our solution

Meet Kim, our new field vet who is straight out of college. She and some of her friends are planning on starting a small initiative called "Vets on Wheels" which focused on patient visits. She and her friends are new to the field and are on a budget.

Meet Dr. Ram. He is a field vet who has been doing this job for the past three years. He has a total patient count of 160. Since he has not been able to find a good solution where he can quickly enter data on the field, he uses Microsoft OneNote to keep track of all his patient records and add details while on the field so that he will not forget any details to enter when he gets home.

Iterations

Wireframing, Prototyping, Usability-Testing & Iterations

Each usability test provided insights, corners, and cases that we had missed. We iterated through 3 versions of sketches, a sketch wireframe for low fidelity, a wireframe, and the high fidelityFigma prototype.

Version 1 - Sketch

Version 2 - Sketch

We verified the two assumptions that we started within the sketch,

  1. The marking of items needed for a visit was not used frequently. While the other to note down animal details as confirmed.
  2. They needed options to know when their next visits were.

Version 3 - Sketch

  1. The first thing the user did when given the dashboard page was to look at the upcoming appointment. So I decided to make the landing page into the upcoming visits page in the coming iterations. Letting the home focus on one element rather than trying to display all.
  2. The users mentioned that they would like to view all the appointments in their Google calendar as well.
  3. When recording a new patient call, they went for entering the owner details first. Contrary to the assumption that the animal details are first to be entered.
  4. Similarly, when searching for an animal, they were more comfortable in finding the owner first searching for the animal by the owner.
  5. Quick create should be accessible from everywhere.

Version 4 - Wireframe

  1. Adding more medical-related details to the animal record.
  2. The layout was designed so that all the details for the visits were on a single page. This caused the users to get confused about how to find a section. So I decided to break down the data entry into chunks and make it into tabs so that navigation between elements will be easier. Easy to find the stuff that you need.
  3. Ability to add all sorts of files as attachments.

Version 5 - High fidelity prototype - 1

  1. The record button did not signify a record. Changed the button.
  2. The users were not able to find out where the recording and the attachments for each visit get stored. So we provided a clear tab to show where they are stored.
  3. The appointment shown on the home page opens to the respective visit.
  4. The users were trying to find out where they can tally out their daily income. So we added an account section under user so that the user can see their billing history.
  5. Allowing the user to choose whether to schedule a visit or start it now from the quick create option. So that the users have a clear distinction of the next action they need to perform. Similarly explicitly allows closing a visit.

Version 6 - High fidelity prototype - 2

  1. The visual design UI lacked finish.
  2. Many of the screens and calls to action were really confusing. Changed a lot of screens to be more easier to understand and navigate.

My Learnings

Some of the things that I learnt while working on this project.

  1. Talking to clients and seeing them use the product is different: For user research, the more you watch what the users use and do, the more you find their problems. It's way better than just doing a user interview. During the initial interview, the users were mostly talking about how they are not able to keep track of their patient records, but while observing them, we were able to find the additional problems in inventory, animal history, and recording of data.
  2. Articulate design decisions: When the clients are people who are in a field that has nothing to do with UX or technology. You need to better articulate your design decisions to make them understand.
  3. Fast paced research: Doctors especially on the field vets are very busy people. Its hard pressed to get their time for user testing. So we need to be prepared fully and conduct the user testing real fast.
