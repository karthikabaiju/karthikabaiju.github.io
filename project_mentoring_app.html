Team Project - 2020

Mentoring App

Microsoft

Overview

When I had joined as a new hire many years ago, one of the main issues I faced was that I didn't have a mentor. Since I was new to Microsoft, I didn't know many people or how to approach people for mentorship. I have faced the same challenge as I started learning UX.

Finding a mentor inside the organization who can help me was difficult.

When my team came up with the idea of creating a mentoring app that allows you to find a mentor, I was more than happy to join the team and start on the problem.

The Challenge

Finding a mentor was based on personal connection

If someone wanted to find a mentor, they had to search in their network to find someone who has the experience in the area you seek mentorship. If there is no one in your network, you will need to be introduced to the potential new mentor by a reliable source. This process tends to be time-consuming and increases the chances of not finding the help required.

No coherent platform existed yet for mentoring or keeping track of your mentoring sessions

There are some platforms, online well as in-person, that help you connect with people for mentorship. But post the initial connection, people find it hard to keep track of their mentoring sessions and progress.

Research

Talking to users revealed some interesting pain points like difficulty in finding a good mentor and keeping tabs on sessions and progress.

I conducted user interviews with many employees. Talking to them made me realize that finding the correct mentor was only half the problem. For mentoring sessions that already existed, they faced issues like not being able to arrange the mentoring session and seeing the progress. For new sessions, they found it difficult to reach out to people and find a mentor that they had a rapport. Many talked about requiring mentoring when newly hired, when learning a new skill and when changing roles. From the user interviews, I was able to create an experience map and categorize them into three main scenarios:

  1. Finding a mentor
  2. Organizing mentoring session
  3. Experimental 1:1 session before long term commitment

Some insights on what the users had to say during the research.

The most consistent opinion was on finding a mentor for your area of interest. The ease of finding such a mentor was the top request.

“If I am a PM looking for a design mentor, better to have a PM with design skills so they can help me in knowing how much design knowledge I would need.”

Role-based mentorship was also something people were interested in. If they are looking for a role change or want to know the future of their role, they want someone who went through that journey to mentor them.

"If I am changing my role, I need a mentor who went through the same transition."

Everyone wanted to choose their mentors and not have them auto-assigned or chosen for them. They want to know about the mentor, what they are offering in the mentorship, and their available time before deciding on a mentor.

"Prefer to have a choice. In case I don't know who to choose, then auto-assign."

"Scheduling the timing that works for both mentor and mentee."

They want a place where they can easily access their mentorship status and organize resources and conversations. A dedicated place of collaboration with the mentor.

"See my mentorship status and activities at a single place."

Users wanted to ensure that they have a good rapport with the mentor, so they want the option to either continue or terminate the mentorship after the first meeting.

"Trial talk to understand if the mentor is on the same page as a mentee."

"Ensure that the first meeting scheduled between the mentor and mentee occurs."

Ideation

How would the user journey for this problem look like?

After the user interview, we regrouped over Microsoft Teams and conducted an in-depth discussion on how we are going to proceed with the functionalities of the mentoring app, how the steps were going to look for the mentor, mentee, and the system. We focused on the flow for getting everything ready and having the first 1:1 mentoring session with a new mentor. We were able to identify the major touchpoints of the user flow.

Recommendations: Get a mentor recommendation for available mentors for the topic they selected.


The mentee should have the option to state their preference on who they would like to be their mentor: Request gets sends to the mentor.


The connection created: If the mentor feels that they will be able to help the mentee, then they will accept the request. A dedicated channel is created for this mentor-mentee collaboration.


First meeting: The first meeting is automatically scheduled and set up.


Option to reject/accept the mentorship for further advancements: Both the mentor and the mentee will have the option to either reject or accept the mentorship after their first meeting according to whether they find the mentorship relation useful. If they accept the request, then the long term schedule will be set up.

The main characteristic of the persona was someone who wanted to learn something new and was looking for a mentor that could guide them in that path.

Iterations

Wireframing, Prototyping, Usability-Testing & Iterations

We iterated through multiple versions of the app before finally settling on one to create a PowerApps prototype. Each usability test provided insights, corners and cases that we had missed. We iterated through 3 versions of sketches, a sketch wireframe for low fidelity, low fidelity prototype and finally the mid fidelity Figma prototype.

During the user testing of the app-based version of the iteration, one of the users mentioned that all the functions that we were trying to accomplish can be easily done through Microsoft Teams. He also asked why we needed to take the data out to another platform when everything could just as easily be done in Teams. Since the default application that any employee at Microsoft opens first thing at the start of the workday is Teams. For any new hires, the first application they get on-boarded to and learn to use is Teams, so we decided to use the teams and channel function available in Microsoft Teams as the base for our application.

My Learnings

Some of the things that I learnt while working on this project.

  1. Iteration makes things better: The solution gets better with each iteration that you run. Your solution will go from "this won't work" to "when can I start using it" with each iteration with the user.
  2. Sketch prototype is as good as Figma: Having multiple sketch prototypes allows you to go through different solutions fast and easily. You won't feel bad about changing the whole layout when things are in the sketch.
  3. Breakthrough suggestions on technology can come from the user as well: We were planning on going with an app for this, then a user suggested that we can have most of the function within Microsoft Teams. Teams already have most of the functionality that we were trying to create and we do not have to build from scratch.
