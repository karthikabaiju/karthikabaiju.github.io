Team Project - 2020

Mentoring App

Microsoft

Upon joining Microsoft as a new hire, the absence of a mentor posed a significant challenge for the individual. Unfamiliar with the organization and lacking connections, they struggled to find someone who could guide them, both during their initial days and as they delved into a new field. This problem space lead us to embark on an exciting project during the Microsoft 2020 Hackathon—the odevelopment of a mentoring app.


Recognizing the transformative power of mentorship, we set out to create a user-friendly app that would streamline the process of finding suitable mentors within the organization. We understood the critical role mentors play in supporting new hires, helping them excel in their roles, and nurturing their growth. With a shared passion for improving the onboarding experience and fostering a culture of learning, our team was determined to make a positive impact.

Team

Team of 6 consisting of Project Manager, Engineering Manager, Developers and Designer

Role

UX Research, Synthesis, Ideation, Prototyping, Visual Design, User Testing

The Challenge

Finding a mentor was based on personal connection

The process of finding a mentor often involves searching within one's network for individuals who possess the desired experience in the area where mentorship is sought. However, when no suitable mentor is present within the existing network, it becomes necessary to be introduced to potential mentors by a reliable source. Unfortunately, this approach can be time-consuming and may ultimately lead to difficulties in finding the necessary guidance and support.

No coherent platform existed yet for mentoring or keeping track of your mentoring sessions

There are some platforms, online well as in-person, that help you connect with people for mentorship. But post the initial connection, people find it hard to keep track of their mentoring sessions and progress.

Research

Talking to users revealed some interesting pain points like difficulty in finding a good mentor and keeping tabs on sessions and progress.

Based on extensive user interviews with numerous employees, it became evident that finding the right mentor was only part of the challenge. The interviews revealed that existing mentoring sessions often encountered issues related to arranging sessions and tracking progress. In the case of new sessions, individuals found it difficult to establish a connection with potential mentors and establish a rapport. The need for mentoring was emphasized during various stages, such as when newly hired, when acquiring new skills, and when transitioning into different roles.

Drawing insights from these interviews, an experience map was developed, categorizing the challenges into three primary scenarios.

Some insights on what the users had to say during the research.

The most consistent opinion was on finding a mentor for your area of interest. The ease of finding such a mentor was the top request.

“If I am a PM looking for a design mentor, better to have a PM with design skills so they can help me in knowing how much design knowledge I would need.”

Role-based mentorship was also something people were interested in. If they are looking for a role change or want to know the future of their role, they want someone who went through that journey to mentor them.

"If I am changing my role, I need a mentor who went through the same transition."

Everyone wanted to choose their mentors and not have them auto-assigned or chosen for them. They want to know about the mentor, what they are offering in the mentorship, and their available time before deciding on a mentor.

"Prefer to have a choice. In case I don't know who to choose, then auto-assign."

"Scheduling the timing that works for both mentor and mentee."

They want a place where they can easily access their mentorship status and organize resources and conversations. A dedicated place of collaboration with the mentor.

"See my mentorship status and activities at a single place."

Users wanted to ensure that they have a good rapport with the mentor, so they want the option to either continue or terminate the mentorship after the first meeting.

"Trial talk to understand if the mentor is on the same page as a mentee."

"Ensure that the first meeting scheduled between the mentor and mentee occurs."

Ideation

How would the user journey for this problem look like?

Following the user interviews, the team regrouped and engaged in a thorough discussion regarding the functional aspects of the mentoring app. The focus was on designing the step-by-step process for mentors and mentees. Special attention was given to the flow of actions required to prepare for and conduct the initial one-on-one mentoring session with a new mentor.

During this discussion, the team successfully identified the key touchpoints within the user flow, which served as major milestones in the mentoring journey. These touchpoints were pivotal in ensuring a smooth and seamless experience for both mentors and mentees using the app.

Recommendations:

Get a mentor recommendation for available mentors for the topic they selected.


Select mentor:

The mentee should have the capability to review the details of available mentors and select the one they prefer. Once the mentee has made their choice, a mentorship request will be sent to the selected mentor with time and date details for the first session.


First meeting:

Upon the mentor accepting the mentorship request, the first meeting will be automatically scheduled for the specific time and date mentioned in the request.

The main characteristic of the persona was someone who wanted to learn something new and was looking for a mentor that could guide them in that path.

During our user research process, we delved deeper into identifying the target persona we wanted to focus on. Through this exploration, we were able to sketch out a well-defined persona. The primary characteristics of this persona revolved around their eagerness to learn something new and their active search for a mentor who could provide guidance on that particular path. This persona became the focal point for our app development, ensuring that our features and functionalities align with the needs and goals of individuals seeking knowledge and mentorship.

Iterations

Wireframing, Prototyping, Usability-Testing & Iterations

Throughout the design process, we iterated through multiple versions to refine the user experience. Usability tests provided valuable insights and highlighted missed areas. We went through three versions of sketches, a low-fidelity wireframe, a low-fidelity prototype, and finally a mid-fidelity Figma prototype for testing and improvement.

Sketch

During user testing of the app-based iteration, one user expressed that all the desired functions could be easily accomplished within Microsoft Teams. They questioned the need to transfer data to another platform when Teams already serves as the default application for Microsoft employees. Considering that Teams is typically the first application employees open at the start of their workday and is commonly used during onboarding, we decided to utilize the Teams and channel functionality as the foundation for our application.

My Learnings

Some of the things that I learnt while working on this project.

  1. Iteration makes things better: The solution gets better with each iteration that you run. Your solution will go from "this won't work" to "when can I start using it" with each iteration with the user.
  2. Sketch prototype is as good as Figma: Having multiple sketch prototypes allows you to go through different solutions fast and easily. You won't feel bad about changing the whole layout when things are in the sketch.
  3. Breakthrough suggestions on technology can come from the user as well: We were planning on going with an app for this, then a user suggested that we can have most of the function within Microsoft Teams. Teams already have most of the functionality that we were trying to create and we do not have to build from scratch.
